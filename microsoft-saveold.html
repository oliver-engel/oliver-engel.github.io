Hacking STEM is a library of Arduino-based learning activities created by Microsoft Education. I helped design, develop, and test a potential new entry to their catalog.

Our response was the Trebuchet Trials: a 4th-grade STEM activity about potential and kinetic energy. Teams of three students use simple circuitry and cardstock templates to build a functioning trebuchet. By connecting an accelerometer and Arduino, students can visualize how potential energy is transformed into kinetic energy.

The activity aligns with Next Generation Science Standards (NGSS) and costs roughly $4 per student.

The trebuchet, Arduino, and digital interface

The functioning prototype uses an interactive data workbook informed by testing with children and teachers.

We used co-design methods and classroom testing thoughout development to inform lesson plan design, our physical form, and the digital interface.

Students are encouraged to change the weights used, craft their own projectiles, and work through challenges that test their knowledge of energy transformation.

I took ownership of both the physical computing and digital interaction design of the system, using P5.js and the Arduino IDE to create a functioning experiment.

We started with rapid ideation and testing with kids to start down-selecting our concepts.

A DIY speaker, hydroelectric turbine, genetically breeding robotic cats... our initial ideation and research aimed to expand our knowledge of the STEM education space. Concepts were developed by selecting interesting NGSS standards and devising a digital-analog experiment to teach that standard. Inspiration was also gathered from pioneers like Mitchel Resnick, who espouses a constructivist approach to STEM education (essentially, learning by experiencing).

We honed in on three of our ideas: a trebuchet, a speaker, and a hydroelectric turbine, and put physical and video prototypes in front of kids to get their feedback and design ideas.

The trebuchet was a hit with kids. But our Microsoft contact had reservations–would a classroom full of trebuchets cause chaos?

We decided to move forward with the trebuchet, but to frame our design around stakeholder buy-in. We knew that the concept was engaging and educational–and also that teachers may not want to take on the risk of flying projectiles in the classroom. We needed to find a balance between the needs of teachers, students, and Microsoft Education.

We also needed to start fleshing out the digital interface–our first step was conducting a Wizard of Oz (WOZ) test to simulate the relationship between the trebuchet, sensor, and the computer.

Early prototype testing was aimed at gauging age appropriateness, feasibility in the classroom, and educational value of the concept.

  • A set of Wizard of Oz tests revealed weaknesses in the relationship between the trebuchet and the digital interface.

We initially included a paper worksheet to track data that was calculated by the interface. In all of our tests, the worksheet was quickly forgotten as participants focused on the trebuchet. We moved towards integrating historical data tracking into the interface.

  • We brought our experiment into an elementary school classroom with 18 kids to see if it would actually descend into chaos.

We were surprised to find... that it didn't. We had kids assemble a laser-cut trebuchet, make their own projectiles, and begin experimenting; they were clearly engaged by the activity and demonstrated understanding of how the variables affected one another.
