Red Share is an augmented social media experience for today's political climate.


March, 1983. The country is rife with red-eyed paranoia–Russia fever presents American society with an existential threat to its established order. You, a DARPA scientist, have just been assigned to the Strategic Computing Initiative, where you are working on an advanced network of artificial intelligences. The network is comprised of bots that assume real identities and reflect human interactions so that they may studied in vitro. Today, you log into the system and learn that it's been compromised.

Relying on multiple levels of anachronism–through content, concept, and aesthetic–Red Share draws a parallel between today's collective paranoia and that of the 1980s. Viewers log in to their personal Facebook accounts and are met with a site that resembles the social media that they're used to, but the form is corrupted. Friends and family post propaganda, the screen glitches and text changes almost at random. The calming blue and white interface is injected with violent streaks of red. The experience is both familiar and alien, meant to be unnerving and humorous; 80's cultural references are swirled together with memes and baby photos, with sometimes shocking or delusional captions.

Red Share was inspired by my personal engagement with socialist politics.


The chaos and utter stupidity of our current political landscape pushed me in 2017 to find solace in socialist ideology as a response to emerging American fascism. The Trump presidency is a manifestation of decades of chauvinism, racism, and hatred of the poor that has finally boiled over and threatens to spill over into our established order. Yet many of us are suspended in self-delusion–conspiracy theory has become mainstream, with every major news program blaring coverage on the evil Russian Empire every minute of the day. Russia, we believe, is the cause of our problems; Russia has infiltrated our society and corrupted our Shining City upon a Hill! (There's no way our problems are ours, and that our current system is haunted by unaddressed strutural pathologies that will continue to divide us). Russia is our scapegoat, so that we don't have to take any real action.

Social media has shifted the way we process current events, but a parallel can be drawn to the hysteria of the 80s... conspiracies flow freely and are amplified by echo-chambers of like-minded people (and a few bots). But our discourse is fake.

The work exists as both a Google Chrome Extension and an installation.


Red Share is a Google Chrome Extension, written in Javascript with the help of the News API and P5.js. It works by identifying text in Facebook posts, and swapping it out for content that's sure to be divisive: alt-right news headlines and anonymous reddit comments are some of the sources. Some of the original text is left intact, creating a disorienting mix of strange propoganda mixed with posts from friends and family.

In its installation form, Red Share is presented on an old CRT TV hooked up to a computer; there's a horrible high-pitched whine from the tubes, and the installation has an 80s aesthetic. Mundane objects are painted black, an aesthetic reference to Louise Nevelson, giving the desk an ominous aesthetic that focuses attention onto the monitor.
