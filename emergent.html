App Concept & UX/UI
Emergent

Global warming has made natural disasters more frequent and more severe.

In 2017, I watched helplessly as major hurricanes, wildfires, and other extreme weather events ravaged the United States and virtually every other area of the world. But I also witnessed a new development in the response to these disasters: people are taking to social media more than ever before in times of need. Residents would tweet out their locations, and others would organize rescue teams on Facebook and Slack, creating a new kind of emergent effort to save human lives and provide resources to victims.

These informal support systems are here to stay. People have always formed emergent groups, and social media is beginning to transform the way they're organized. I began with the premise of creating a scaffolding for these groups‚Äìto connect people in need to people who want to help, in a centralized platform that more efficiently saves human lives during disasters.



  • Create a mobile phone application that efficiently connects people who need relief to people with the means to help them
  • Provide tangible value during and after natural disasters, no matter how many people use the platform
  • Facilitate communication between formal institutions (like the Coast Guard & National Guard) and these emergent groups to better organize rescue & relief efforts


From my close reading of news reports on Hurricane Harvey, I knew that there was a software programmer named Greg Sadetsky who had created an interactive platform for the Coast Guard to track people who needed to be rescued. I reached out to Sadetsky for a short interview about his process, tools, and design decisions. I received an immense amount of important resources‚Äìfrom actual volunteer speadsheets to useful Javascript map APIs. I collected some insights from Sadetsky:


  • The Coast Guard interface started off with simple request locations, but as rescue efforts began, it became clear that more information was needed to efficiently help people. Sadetsky ended up adding hospital locations, no-fly zones, other location-specific information that was arranged in toggleable layers via a side panel UI.
  • The volunteer tasks were segmented into rescue efforts and relief efforts. The relief efforts were further segmented into: shelter, animal care, medical aid, pharmacy aid, food distribution, resource distribution, volunteer opportunities, and other.
  • Twitter and Facebook were the most important social media platforms for the organization of rescue and relief efforts.

I also did research into the dynamics of emergent groups during crises and the most common types of natural disasters in the US.



I started on paper, splitting the app into three distinct user interfaces: 1) for those who need help, 2) for those that can give help, and 3) for those that want to add to the local communal map, either resources or incidents. Taking after Sadetsky's work, the main focus of the app is the map, which features resource and incident markers, as well as the locations of those who need rescuing and the rescuers.
