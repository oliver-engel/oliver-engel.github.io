App Concept & UX/UI
Emergent

Global warming has made natural disasters more frequent and more severe.

In 2017, I watched helplessly as major hurricanes, wildfires, and other extreme weather events ravaged the United States and virtually every other area of the world. But I also witnessed a new development in the response to these disasters: people are taking to social media more than ever before in times of need. Residents would tweet out their locations, and others would organize rescue teams on Facebook and Slack, creating a new kind of emergent effort to save human lives and provide resources to victims.

These informal support systems are here to stay. People have always formed emergent groups, and social media is beginning to transform the way they're organized. I began with the premise of creating a scaffolding for these groups‚Äìto connect people in need to people who want to help, in a centralized platform that more efficiently saves human lives during disasters.

Emergent provides a platform for localized disaster support.

The Challenge
  • Create a mobile phone application that efficiently connects people who need relief to people with the means to help them
  • Provide tangible value during and after natural disasters, no matter how many people use the platform
  • Facilitate communication between formal institutions (like the Coast Guard & National Guard) and these emergent groups to better organize rescue & relief efforts
Process
  • Research similar products to determine important functions, types of information to provide, UI features, and general aesthetics..
  • Use sketching on paper and digital wireframing to establish basic information architecture and interaction flows.
  • Refine wireframes into working prototypes, create animations to give a sense of what the interactive features and motion design will look like.


01. Research

  Some organizing documents used for rescue and relief efforts during the hurricane.

Secondary Interview

From my close reading about rescue efforts post-Hurricane Harvey, I knew that there was a software engineer named Greg Sadetsky who had worked directly with citizen "first responders" and the Coast Guard to create more formalized rescue strategies. I knew he would be a great source of insight into the process, and I reached out to ask him some questions regarding his experience with the projects, what he learned from the people he worked with, and what he think should be done in the future.

I gleaned some valuable insights from Sadetsky:

  • Providing a communal map where people could report locations of available resources was a feature that people actually used and requested.
  • The volunteer tasks were segmented into rescue efforts and relief efforts. The relief efforts were further segmented into things like shelter, animal care, medical aid, etc.
  • The communal map was beneficial to both civilians and formal government institutions, and allowed the Coast Guard to get a better sense of what was actually happening on the ground.
Examining Existing Platforms

Sadetsky also provided me with a trove of relevant organizations, raw data, and APIs that were working towards similar goals. I spent time studying materials like the actual resource spreadsheets set up by volunteer groups, in order to inform the way I structured the app. I also did research into the formation of emergent groups and the relative prevalence of natural disaster types in the US.

While designing my app, I wanted to be sure that I considered the actual technical limitations of such a system. Sadetsky highly recommended the Google Maps API, which was able to efficiently perform all the the necessary location and area marking that his platform required.


02. Ideation

  Excerpts from ideation notes.

Determining Function Necessity

One goal of the app is to provide a scaffolding for interaction as opposed to dictating certain user flows. This requires the app to connect the most relevant raw tools into an easy-to-use interface, and to strip away whatever doesn't meet that standard.

Here's a list of the main functions I wanted to include:

  • People in need ("Requesters") can either submit a request for rescue, or a request to have a welfare check performed on someebody that they are unable to reach.
  • People who can help ("Rescuers") are able to see a visualization of the requesters around them, and via a list UI are able to view additional information about the request, like how many people are in the group, how long ago the request was made, and the distance to the requester.
  • Rescuers and requesters should be able to contact each other via phone. Rescuers should also be able to see other rescuers, and requesters should be able to see other requesters. The app should facilitate emergent groups, like rescuers banding together or requesters coordinating shelter spaces.
  • Anybody ("Reporters") should be able to report on-the-ground hazards and community resources by marking them on a communal map. This could include power outages, flooded areas, or resources like available shelters and water distribution centers.
Splitting the UI

From my list of functions, I decided to separate the mobile app into three different UI views (the view is determined during onboarding, and is based on the immediate action that the user would like to perform with the app). That means there is a separate (but similar) UI set for Rescuers, Requesters, and Reporters, which prioritizes the functions that are most valuable to each user type. Some users will certainly be hybrids, like a rescuer who needs to make a rescue request‚Äìthese crossover functions will be available in a hamburger menu, so that every user type has the ability to perform the actions of another user type.

Rescuer UI

Prioritize the lists of nearby requesters and rescuers, and also a map filtering system that allows for a quick understanding of the immediate landscape so they can plan rescue missions.


Requester UI

Like the rescuer UI, prioritize the lists of nearby rescuers and request, and also a map filtering system. Requesters should be able to easily update their requests to reflect quickly-changing situations.


Reporter UI

The reporter should be able to quickly view surrounding conditions via map filters and add and delete reports as they are relevant.

Insights from Wireframing

After completing my wireframes, I showed them to a few friends for feedback.

  • The map filtering function felt too different from the other options in the bottom sliding menu‚Äìso I moved the filtering option to a standalone button in the upper left corner of the screen.
  • I turned the rescuer, requester, and report lists into card interfaces that can be navigated by swiping (see the first animation). This borrows from popular apps currently on the market and makes the information more easily accessible.
  • I originally designed the bottom sliding menu to have three different states, either minimized, 40% height, or 80% height, which could be navigated with a vertical swipe. While each view had a different amount of information, the 40% height didn't have enough value and added an extra swipe to the interaction, so I removed it.


04. Aesthetic & Final Development

Onboarding

Onboarding should be rapid & secure. The signup process can be completed via Facebook or through phone verification. In the setup, I give the user three options: make a request, join the effort, or become a reporter, with a short description of what each means. Their selection here determines which UI their app is set up with. In case the user wants to go straight to the map, there's an option to skip the process entirely via the "view map" link.

The user can immediately access the function that they've signed up to use, or also have the option to slide the menu down and go straight to the map.

Main Functions

Emergent provides a centralized communication tool that elucidates a person's immediate environment during the uncertainty of disaster situations. This circumvents the inefficiency of using Facebook and Twitter, which muddles the important information in lengthy spreadsheets, and is typically only accessed by rescuers.

The User Profile

Rescuers and requesters each have a profile card with quick information about their request or ability to help, and actions to either call, message, or view that person on the map. The profiles are displayed in both a list view and a card view that can be swiped left or right to see a new profile. Profiles are automatically sorted via "best match", which compares the data on each user to determine who would most efficiently be able to help. Criteria for best match are based on distance, time last active, number of people in the requester's group, and capacity of the rescuer's vehicle, if available.


Match Indicator

A dot on the left side of the Rescuer's name quickly indicates the quality of the match‚Äìeither green, orange, or red. The requester can contact anybody, regardless of the match quality, so the indicator is meant only to help make that choice more efficient.

Requester & Rescuer Tools

Requesters and rescuers can search for other nearby rescuers and requesters, edit their requests, and view a list or search from nearby resources.

Reporter Tools

Reporters can view a list of their reports, search for rescuers and requesters, and submit new reports.

Quick Actions

Tapping the hamburger menu gives all users the same menu interface, which contains a set of universal actions, messages, and a further option (the gear) to open up the app's settings.

  • Make a Report: opens an interface to file a report for a resource or incident
  • Make a Request: opens an interface to make a request for rescue or for a welfare check
  • Join a Group: allows any user to connect with multiple other users to centralize communication when acting in a group
  • Nearest Hospitals: provides a list of the nearest hospitals and connects
  • Find Resources: opens an interface to view and filter nearby resources
  • Share Emergent: opens a share dialogue that lets the user post about Emergent on social media to encourage others to communicate using the platform
